Complete Guide to Upper Mustang Trekking 2026

For decades, Upper Mustang was sealed from the outside world — a medieval Tibetan kingdom frozen in time behind restricted borders. Today it's open, but access remains controlled by permit and price. That's exactly what preserved it. Lo Manthang is the closest thing to ancient Tibet that still exists on earth.

Share:

There are still places in Nepal that feel genuinely other — not exotic in a theme-park sense, but actually different, as if the landscape and the people inhabiting it follow rules that were written long before you arrived and will continue long after you leave. Upper Mustang is one of those places. The high desert plateau north of the Annapurna range receives almost no monsoon rain. The cliffs are ochre and rust and chalk-white. The villages are walled. The monastery courtyards are older than many European nations.

Upper Mustang was closed to outsiders until 1992. For most of the 20th century it functioned as a semi-autonomous kingdom — the Kingdom of Lo — with its own royal family, its own customs, its own version of Tibetan Buddhism, and essentially no contact with the outside world. The permit system that controls access today isn't just bureaucracy. It's the reason the place still looks the way it does.

Nepal's Last Restricted Kingdom

Upper Mustang is one of Nepal's few remaining restricted trekking areas. Access requires a Restricted Area Permit costing $500 per person for the first 10 days, then $50 per additional day — the most expensive trekking permit in Nepal. Groups must consist of at least two trekkers and be accompanied by a licensed government trekking guide at all times. This is not optional. The restrictions have kept visitor numbers low and the landscape — and culture — remarkably intact. Full permit breakdown →

What Makes Upper Mustang Different

Most trekking routes in Nepal take you through lush, green, forested landscapes that gradually open into alpine terrain. Upper Mustang does the opposite. Once you cross the Kagbeni checkpoint and enter the restricted zone, the vegetation thins out and within a day the world turns into something that looks borrowed from the Tibetan Plateau — deep-carved river canyons, fluted cliffs in shades of red and gold, and villages that appear almost organically grown from the earth they stand on.

The Kali Gandaki Gorge — the deepest river gorge on earth — is your approach corridor. You trek north alongside it, into increasingly arid, wind-scoured terrain, until you arrive at Lo Manthang: the walled capital of the Kingdom of Lo, population around 1,000, home to four functioning monasteries, a 15th-century royal palace, and some of the best-preserved Tibetan Buddhist art in the Himalayan world.

A Living Tibetan Kingdom

Nepal abolished its monarchy in 2008, but the King of Lo — Jigme Dorje Palbar Bista — remains the cultural figurehead of Upper Mustang and is still addressed as king by local people. The 2011 Losar ceremonies marked the formal end of the kingdom, but Lo Manthang operates with a degree of cultural continuity that is almost impossible to find elsewhere. When you walk through the walled city, people are living inside a medieval streetplan that has existed for six hundred years. That's the whole point of going.

Visualising Your Journey North

Upper Mustang is a linear trek — you follow the Kali Gandaki valley north to Lo Manthang, then retrace your steps south. The elevation profile is surprisingly gentle compared to most Nepal treks: no major high passes, no technical sections. What the route delivers instead is a daily visual transformation — the landscape changes dramatically with every 500m you gain, and by the time you reach Lo Manthang, the world looks completely unlike anything at the start of the walk.

The Wind Factor

The Kali Gandaki is the world's deepest gorge, and it acts as a wind funnel. From late morning onwards — typically 11:00 to 14:00 — the gorge channels a powerful south-to-north wind that can make trekking genuinely unpleasant and occasionally dangerous on exposed ridges. Every experienced guide on this route starts walking by 06:30–07:00. By the time the wind kicks in, you should be at your destination or close to it. Ignore this and you will regret it.

When to Go — and Why This Trek Is Different

Upper Mustang has a quality that makes it unique among Nepal's trekking regions: it's the only major trek in Nepal that is excellent during the monsoon. Because the Annapurna and Dhaulagiri ranges shield the plateau from monsoon rain, Upper Mustang receives minimal rainfall from June to September — the season when most other Nepal treks are muddy, leech-ridden, and often completely washed out.

Spring (March to May) — The Classic Season

Cool temperatures, clear skies, and the dramatic contrast of a green-ish lower valley against the ochre desert plateau above. March brings cold nights in Lo Manthang but excellent visibility. May is warmer and dust can increase in the afternoons. Our preferred spring window is late March through mid-April.

Monsoon (June to September) — The Hidden Season

If you want fewer trekkers and don't mind some afternoon cloud, June through early September in Upper Mustang is genuinely good. The permit system already limits numbers; the monsoon reduces them further. Accommodation is easier to arrange, teahouses are less crowded, and the landscape takes on an otherworldly quality with storm light over the cliffs. July and August are the best monsoon months — avoid early June when residual cold and unsettled weather persist.

Autumn (October to November)

Nepal's peak trekking season brings more visitors but also the most reliable weather window. October in Upper Mustang is outstanding — cold nights, brilliant days, and good visibility to the Annapurna and Nilgiri peaks to the south. Accommodation books out faster in autumn; arrange in advance.

December to February — Avoid

Winter in Upper Mustang is extremely cold (Lo Manthang regularly drops to –20°C at night), many teahouses close entirely, and the jeep road north of Kagbeni becomes impassable after snowfall. Some experienced winter trekking teams do the route in January with full camping equipment and cold-weather gear, but this is not a standard tourist itinerary. Beyond Summit does not run group departures December through February.

15-Day Upper Mustang Itinerary

The following is Beyond Summit's standard group itinerary. The route north takes 5–6 days; two full days in Lo Manthang (minimum) are non-negotiable if you're going to understand the place at all; the return is 4–5 days. The jeep road running parallel to the trail means that sick or injured trekkers can be evacuated by vehicle to Jomsom on most sections of the route — a significant safety advantage over more remote treks.

Phase 1 — Getting There
Day1

Kathmandu → Pokhara → Jomsom (2,720m)

Flight to Pokhara (1hr), then the 20-minute mountain flight to Jomsom — one of the more dramatic airport approaches in the world, with the Nilgiri and Tilicho massifs visible from your window. The Jomsom flight is weather-dependent and morning-only; delays are common, particularly in spring when cloud builds quickly. Always fly Kathmandu–Pokhara the night before to buffer the Jomsom leg. Afternoon in Jomsom to acclimatise, check permits, and attend the Beyond Summit briefing.

Jomsom → Kagbeni (2,810m)

An easy 2–3 hour walk north along the Kali Gandaki riverbed to Kagbeni. Start early to avoid the afternoon wind — the riverbed walk is exposed. Kagbeni is your first taste of what's ahead: whitewashed houses, prayer flags on every rooftop, a blood-red monastery perched on the ridge above town. The official RAP checkpoint is here; your government-licensed guide handles the paperwork. This is where Upper Mustang technically begins.

The landscape transforms completely on this day. Leave Kagbeni's green edges behind and enter the high desert. The trail crosses the Kali Gandaki on a suspension bridge and begins climbing through increasingly dramatic canyon terrain. By Chele — a tiny village of stone houses clinging to a cliff shelf — you are in a landscape unlike anything you've seen on the walk so far. Chele → Syangboche → Ghiling (3,570m)

The hardest trekking day of the route — a sustained ascent to the Syangboche ridge at around 3,800m before dropping into the wide Ghiling valley. The views from the top are sweeping: layers of arid ridges stacked to the north, and on a clear morning, the Annapurna massif floating to the south. Ghiling is a tidy, prosperous village; local men play cards outside the teahouses in the evenings with the kind of unhurried calm that feels like it belongs to another century.

Day5

Ghiling → Ghami (3,795m)

A gentler day that moves through increasingly Tibetan-feeling terrain — prayer wheels, juniper-framed trail sections, and the dramatic approach to Ghami across a wide, flat plain. The famous Ghami mani wall runs along the edge of the village — walk clockwise, always. The monastery above the village has a small, active monk community; the abbot is usually willing to speak with trekkers who arrive respectfully and at appropriate hours.

↑LO

Ghami → Lo Manthang (3,840m)

The arrival at Lo Manthang is one of the great moments of Himalayan trekking. You come over a small ridge and the walled city appears below you — compact, golden-white, completely intact. The outer wall of the city is original 15th-century construction. The main gate is still the only vehicle entry point. Inside: narrow streets, whitewashed alleyways, children returning from school, and the faint smell of incense from four monastery courtyards running simultaneously. First afternoon is for walking the streets and letting the place sink in.

Lo Manthang — Monastery Day

Full day inside and around the walled city. Morning visits to Thubchen Gompa (the finest murals), Jampa Lhakhang (the oldest building), and Chodey monastery. The mural restoration project in Thubchen has been ongoing since the 1990s with funding from international conservation bodies — the rescued frescoes rival anything in Bhutan or Ladakh. Afternoon: the Namgyal Gompa north of the city walls, and if timing allows, the royal palace courtyard (not open to the public interior, but the courtyard is accessible).

What Kind of Trek Is This, Honestly?

Upper Mustang is classified as a moderate trek. That classification is accurate, but it needs a few qualifications.

No technical climbing, no glaciers, no crampons. The route is entirely on trail — sometimes dusty jeep track, sometimes ancient flagstone path, occasionally loose scree traverse on exposed ridge sections. The highest point you walk to on the standard itinerary is Lo Manthang at 3,840m (or Gyu La pass at 4,077m on the optional loop). That's below Everest Base Camp. Compared to most Nepal treks, this is a relatively accessible altitude profile.

The altitude still matters. Some people acclimatise poorly at 3,800m. It's less common than at 5,000m+ altitudes, but it happens. A slow first two days, steady hydration, and not pushing through headaches are the standard protocols. Our itinerary builds in a gradual ascent that mitigates AMS risk significantly.

The wind is the real physical factor. Not altitude, not trail technicality — it's the afternoon wind in the Kali Gandaki gorge that trekkers underestimate. On exposed ridge days it can stop you in your tracks. Starting early eliminates most of this problem.

The remoteness is real. North of Ghami there is no road access, no reliable phone signal, and basic medical care. Your guide's experience and your travel insurance are your two actual safety nets. Both are non-optional on our itineraries.

Right for You If...

Upper Mustang is ideal for trekkers who have already done one or two Nepal treks (Annapurna Circuit, EBC) and want something culturally richer and more remote. It's also excellent for those who can't or don't want to go above 4,000m but still want a genuinely challenging, rewarding trek in a profoundly different environment. Photographers, history enthusiasts, and Buddhist art lovers often consider it the best trek they've ever done. Altitude-seekers after 5,000m+ should look at Mera Peak or EBC instead.

What to Pack — Mustang Specifics

The standard EBC or Annapurna packing list covers most of what you need for Upper Mustang, with a few key differences driven by the desert environment.

The Mustang Additions

  • Windproof shell jacket (essential): A waterproof-only shell won't cut it here. The afternoon winds are powerful and cold even in October. A well-sealed windproof layer is more important on this trek than any other Nepal route we run.
  • Buff or neck gaiter: The dust on the jeep-road sections north of Kagbeni is dense. In spring and autumn, trail dust coats everything and gets into your lungs if you're not covered up. Two buffs minimum.
  • Quality sunglasses (UV400, wrap style): The plateau receives extreme UV at altitude with reflective pale rock and regular direct sun exposure. Your sunglasses from home are probably inadequate. Buy good ones before you leave Kathmandu or Pokhara.
  • Lip balm and heavy moisturiser: The Mustang plateau is extremely dry. SPF lip balm and a thick moisturiser used twice daily will save you significant discomfort. This is not luxury gear — it's practical protection.
  • Trekking poles: Not mandatory, but the loose river-crossing sections and steep descents from Syangboche are significantly easier with poles. Strongly recommended.
  • Layers for Lo Manthang evenings: Night-time temperatures in Lo Manthang drop sharply. Even in summer, you'll want a down jacket for evenings in the teahouse courtyard. Don't underpack for cold — the lodges have minimal heating.
Carry Enough Cash

There are no ATMs north of Jomsom. None. Lo Manthang teahouses, monastery entry fees, and any additional expenses must be paid in Nepali Rupees or USD cash. Budget NPR 15,000–25,000 ($110–185) for meals and incidentals beyond your package, and carry it from Jomsom. Withdrawing from a Jomsom ATM is reliable; from anywhere further north, impossible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I do Upper Mustang independently without a guide?

No. The Restricted Area Permit for Upper Mustang legally requires the presence of a government-licensed guide at all times north of Kagbeni. You cannot obtain the permit independently — it can only be purchased through a registered trekking agency, and inspections do occur at checkpoints. This is not a bureaucratic inconvenience; it's a fundamental part of how access to the region is controlled and conservation funded. Our guides also add significant value — monastery access, cultural interpretation, and safety in a remote area where there are no walk-in medical facilities.

How physically fit do I need to be?

Solid trekking fitness for 5–6 hours per day on uneven trail at 3,000–3,800m. You don't need to have done a Nepal trek before, but you should be comfortable walking for full days in hilly terrain with a daypack. Prior altitude experience above 3,000m is beneficial but not required for Upper Mustang — the altitude ceiling is moderate enough that well-acclimatised trekkers rarely have serious AMS issues.

What is accommodation like in Upper Mustang?

Teahouses throughout the route from Kagbeni to Lo Manthang — simple rooms with twin beds, shared facilities, and communal dining rooms. The standard improves steadily as you approach Lo Manthang. In the capital itself, several teahouses have been upgraded with private bathrooms and better insulation. This is not lodge trekking in the EBC sense — facilities are basic, particularly at Ghiling and Ghami. Hot showers are a pleasant surprise when they appear; plan for cold and be grateful for warm.

Are the monasteries accessible to trekkers?

Yes, with appropriate behaviour and entry fees (typically NPR 500–2,000 per monastery). The four major monasteries of Lo Manthang — Thubchen, Jampa, Chodey, and Choprang — all permit respectful visitors during daylight hours outside of prayer times. Remove shoes at the threshold. Ask your guide before photographing monks, ceremonies, or sacred objects — this is not a context where tourist photography norms apply universally. The murals at Thubchen are the cultural crown jewel of the trek; stand quietly in front of them and give them the time they deserve.

What is the Tiji Festival?

Tiji (Tenchi) is a three-day festival unique to Lo Manthang, held annually in May (date varies with the Tibetan lunar calendar). It involves elaborate masked dance ceremonies enacted by monks in the Namgyal monastery courtyard — a dramatisation of the story of a deity driving away a demon threatening the kingdom. It's one of the great cultural spectacles in the Himalayan world and draws both local families and international visitors. Tiji dates in 2026 fall in late May — contact us if you want to time your trek for the festival. Accommodation in Lo Manthang sells out months in advance during Tiji.
