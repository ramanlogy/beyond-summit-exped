Complete Guide to Mera Peak Climbing 2026

At 6,476 metres, Mera Peak is the highest trekking peak in Nepal. It is also — and this surprises a lot of people — one of the more accessible ones. The upper mountain is a broad, glacier-covered dome. The hard part isn't the climbing. It's getting there.

Share:

The Hinku Valley doesn't get the foot traffic that the Khumbu does. There's no Namche Bazaar with its bakeries and Wi-Fi, no strings of lodges every two hours. The trail into Mera Peak winds through rhododendron forest, past yak pastures and remote villages that see relatively few outsiders. That remoteness is the point — and, for the right kind of person, it's the best part of the whole trip.

What you get at the end of that trail is the highest summit available to trekkers in Nepal without expedition-level technical climbing. Five of the world's fourteen eight-thousanders — Everest, Lhotse, Cho Oyu, Makalu, Kangchenjunga — are all visible from the top on a clear morning. That view is, by any measure, one of the finest things you can see on this planet from under your own power.

Nepal's Highest Trekking Peak

Mera is special in a specific way: it is the highest summit in Nepal classified as a trekking peak, sitting above Island Peak (6,189m) and Lobuche East (6,119m). The upper mountain is a glaciated dome — significantly less technical than its altitude suggests. For a well-prepared, fit trekker, Mera offers something rare: genuine high altitude without the technical barriers that reserve most 6,000m+ peaks for experienced mountaineers. See how it compares →

What Makes Mera Different

Most 6,000m peaks in Nepal fall into one of two categories: either they're genuinely technical — with steep ridges, mixed rock and ice, rappels, and fixed ropes that demand real climbing skills — or they're high-altitude walks dressed up as peaks. Mera Peak is neither. It occupies a rare middle ground.

The North Face normal route is a glacier ascent. Crampons are essential, a rope team is mandatory, and the altitude is serious. But there are no rappels, no technical mixed sections, no near-vertical ice. What you're dealing with above high camp is a steady, grinding snow slope that tops out on a broad plateau — the summit marked by a cairn and, on a clear day, a wall of eight-thousanders that fills the horizon from left to right like a painting nobody would believe if you described it from memory.

Mera Peak was first climbed in 1953 — the same year as Everest — by Jimmy Roberts and Sen Tenzing. It has been a popular trekking peak for decades, but the remoteness of the Hinku Valley approach keeps the numbers manageable. Unlike Island Peak, you won't find a queue on the fixed ropes at 05:00.

Comparing Mera Peak with Island Peak (6,189m) or Lobuche East (6,119m)? Mera is higher than both but technically easier on the upper mountain. The trade-off is a longer, more remote approach through the Hinku Valley. If altitude is your primary goal and you want fewer people around, Mera is the right call. If you want to combine a summit with EBC, go with Lobuche East.

Where Mera Fits in the Picture

Mera is often talked about as a stepping stone — and it is, in the sense that it gives you glacier experience and serious altitude before moving to more technical peaks. But it's also worth treating as a destination in its own right. Not everyone who climbs Mera wants to keep going higher. Some people just want to stand at 6,476m and see five eight-thousanders at once. That's enough.

When to Go

Autumn (October to November)

October is the sweet spot. The monsoon has cleared, the Hinku Valley trails have dried out, and the glacier above Mera La is in its best condition of the year — firm, predictable, well-defined. The views from the summit on an October morning are extraordinary: cold, clear air and no haze on the horizon. Late October through mid-November is the window we run the most groups, and for good reason.

Spring (March to May)

Spring on Mera is quieter than autumn and has its own character. The lower valley fills with rhododendron bloom from late March through April, which makes the approach genuinely beautiful. April on the upper mountain is typically stable, with decent snow conditions on the glacier. Some years the upper slopes carry more wind than autumn; it varies. April is our preferred spring month — May gets warmer and the snow can be heavier and less consolidated.

Avoid the Monsoon Approach

The Hinku Valley trail crosses several steep sections prone to landslide during monsoon (June–September). The glacier above Mera La accumulates unstable wet snow that makes summit attempts genuinely dangerous. Beyond Summit does not operate Mera expeditions during the monsoon window. Winter (December–February) is feasible for experienced cold-weather teams but involves temperatures below –25°C on the upper mountain and is not part of our standard season.

The Route — What You're Actually Climbing

Mera Peak has three summits: Mera Central (6,461m), Mera North (6,476m, the true high point), and Mera South (6,065m). The standard route from the Hinku Valley targets Mera North via a two-camp system on the glacier.

The Approach: Hinku Valley

The approach to Mera base camp is a 5–7 day walk from Lukla through the Hinku Valley. This is the part of the trip that separates Mera from Lobuche or Island Peak. You're not on the EBC highway — you're on quieter trails through Pangkongma, Chutanga, and Kothe, gaining altitude gradually through forest and alpine terrain that most trekkers in Nepal never see. The approach itself is worth the trip.

Base Camp to Mera La (5,415m)

From base camp at Khare (5,045m), the route climbs to Mera La — the high pass that separates the Hinku Valley from the Mera Glacier. This section involves walking on the lower glacier with crampons; it's the point where the trek becomes a climb. Mera La camp sits on a broad, flat platform with front-row views of the upper mountain ahead and the Hinku Valley dropping away behind you. Cold nights, extraordinary skies.

Mera La to High Camp (5,800m) and Summit

Above Mera La, the route crosses the Mera Glacier to a high camp at approximately 5,800m. Summit day departs at 03:00–04:00 and follows the broad North Face snow slope to the summit plateau. There is no genuinely technical crux — the angle is sustained but never extreme. The final few hundred metres to the summit cairn on a clear morning, with Everest, Lhotse, Makalu, Cho Oyu, and Kangchenjunga all visible simultaneously, is one of those things that is hard to describe without sounding like you're exaggerating. You're not.

Summit Day Timing

Aim for the summit between 07:00 and 09:00. The broad summit plateau offers no shelter from the wind — early morning before convective cloud builds over the Hinku Valley gives you the best conditions and the clearest views. A 03:00 departure from high camp is standard. Don't push for speed on the ascent; the summit day is long and the altitude is serious. Slow and steady wins on Mera.

The Hinku Valley Advantage

The 5–7 day approach through the Hinku Valley does something that no amount of deliberate acclimatisation scheduling can fully replicate: it acclimatises you gradually, day by day, through terrain that is beautiful enough to keep your mind off the altitude. By the time you reach Khare at 5,045m, your body has had 8 days to adjust. Summit day is still hard. But the AMS risk is meaningfully lower than on itineraries that rush the approach.

Honest Skills Assessment

Mera is often described as the most accessible high-altitude peak in Nepal, and in terms of technical difficulty on the upper mountain, that's true. What it glosses over is that the glacier travel and the altitude are genuinely serious — they just require a different kind of preparation than technical climbing skill.

  • Crampon walking on glacier: The entire upper route above Mera La is on glaciated terrain requiring crampons. This is different from scrambling on rocky trail — there's a specific walking technique that reduces fatigue and improves safety on snow and ice. Our Kathmandu briefing and a guided practice session at Khare cover this thoroughly.
  • Rope team travel: The glacier sections are travelled as a roped team. The technique is simple to learn and is covered in the pre-departure skills session. Understanding what to do if a team member punches through a snow bridge is the key concept — again, not complicated, just important to know before you're on the glacier.
  • Ice axe self-arrest: Carried on the summit slope; used only if you slip on the upper face. Unlikely if you're moving carefully, but you should know the technique before summit day. Covered in the Khare skills session.
  • Physical stamina at very high altitude: The single most important factor on Mera is how your body handles sustained effort above 5,800m. There is no shortcut to this. Prior experience above 5,000m — whether on an EBC trek, Lobuche, Island Peak, or another major high-altitude route — is the best preparation you can do.
No Prior Climbing Experience Required

Mera Peak can be safely guided for trekkers with no prior mountaineering experience, provided they are physically fit and have completed serious high-altitude trekking (EBC, Annapurna Circuit, or equivalent) beforehand. The technical skills are taught before and during the climb. What cannot be taught in 18 days is cardiovascular fitness and altitude acclimatisation capacity — those need months of preparation before you arrive in Kathmandu.

Getting Ready for Mera

The 6,476m summit demands serious preparation. The good news is that the preparation is straightforward — there's no special equipment to acquire or technique to master before you leave home. What Mera asks for is cardiovascular fitness, specifically the ability to sustain aerobic output at altitude over many consecutive hours.

Start 12 weeks out and build toward these benchmarks:

  • Long days out: 6–8 hours of hiking with 900–1,200m of elevation gain, at least twice a month. These are not casual walks. They should be tiring.
  • Aerobic base: 3–4 sessions per week of running, cycling, rowing, or swimming. Sustained output for 45–60 minutes is the target — not interval training, sustained effort.
  • Weighted descents: Put a 12kg pack on and find the longest, steepest descent near you. Do it until your legs are genuinely tired. Then think about the fact that summit day descent on Mera is longer and at 6,000m. Adjust accordingly.
  • A multi-day trip with overnight camping in the 2–3 months before departure. Sleep deprivation, cooking at camp, and variable weather are all things you want to have dealt with before they're happening on the Mera Glacier.
One More Thing About Fitness

Mera's summit day starts at high camp (5,800m) and gains 676m to the top. At that altitude, the air has roughly 47% of the oxygen available at sea level. Your lungs will be working significantly harder than they ever have. The climbers who struggle most on summit day are almost always the ones who trained for the summit day — but not for the 10 previous days that lead up to it. Build your endurance for sustained multi-day effort, not just for one big single-day push.

What to Bring

Standard EBC trekking kit covers the Hinku Valley approach. Once you hit the glacier above Mera La, you need mountaineering-specific additions. Source or rent these before leaving Kathmandu — reliable rental shops are plentiful in Thamel and our team will steer you to the right ones.

Glacier and Summit Additions

  • Mountaineering boots: Insulated, crampon-compatible, rated to –20°C. Your EBC trekking boots will not work above Mera La. Non-negotiable.
  • 12-point crampons: Fitted to your mountaineering boots and tested before Kathmandu departure. Bring a small screwdriver for on-route adjustments.
  • Ice axe: Standard walking length, for the upper North Face slope. Technique covered in the Khare skills session.
  • Harness: For glacier travel — crevasse rescue and rope team protocol. Fitted comfortably over all your layers. Test with everything on.
  • Prusiks or ascender: For the rare steep fixed rope section near the summit. Beyond Summit supplies these for clients who don't own them.

High Camp

  • Sleeping bag rated to –20°C: High camp at 5,800m with wind chill is genuinely extreme. This is not a place to use your three-season bag with an extra blanket.
  • Down suit or heavyweight down jacket plus insulated trousers: The pre-dawn hours on the summit slope are the coldest part of the trip. Layer correctly.
  • Headtorch with spare batteries: Lithium batteries only at altitude — alkaline batteries lose power rapidly in cold. Two full spare sets minimum.
  • Thermos of hot liquid: Prepared the night before. Hydration on the summit slope matters and the options on the mountain are limited to what you carry.
  • High-SPF sunscreen and glacier glasses: The UV exposure on a snow glacier at 6,000m is severe. Snow blindness is temporary but genuinely awful. Quality glacier glasses (category 4, wraparound) are essential.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I do Mera without doing EBC first?

Yes — Mera is a standalone itinerary through a completely different valley. No EBC experience is required to book a Mera Peak expedition. That said, prior high-altitude experience above 5,000m (on EBC, Lobuche, Island Peak, Kilimanjaro, or a similar route) is strongly recommended and makes a meaningful difference to your summit chances. If you have no prior altitude experience, talk to our team before booking — we'll give you an honest assessment.

What is the summit success rate?

With adequate acclimatisation and good weather, Beyond Summit groups achieve summit rates above 80% on Mera. The most common reasons for turnaround are weather (high wind on the summit plateau), individual AMS symptoms at high camp, and — less commonly — insufficient physical preparation showing up on summit day. Our itinerary is structured to address the first two directly. The third one is on you and requires honest training in the months beforehand.

What happens if there's bad weather on summit day?

We build a one-day weather buffer into the itinerary above high camp — if summit day conditions are marginal, the standard call is to wait 24 hours at high camp and attempt the following morning. Beyond that, the decision to turn back or continue is made jointly by the lead guide and the client group with full transparency about the conditions and risks. Our guides have a combined total of several hundred Mera summits. Their read on the weather is the one that matters.

Is Mera safe on the glacier?

Glacier travel carries inherent risks — crevasse fall being the primary one — which is why rope team travel is mandatory on the Mera Glacier sections. Our guides lead clients through the glacier in roped teams following established and regularly reviewed routes. Crevasse rescue techniques are taught at the Khare skills session. The glacier on Mera's normal route is well-documented and relatively predictable, but no glacier is without risk, which is why rope discipline is non-negotiable.

Do I need helicopter rescue insurance?

Yes, and it's a condition of our package. Emergency evacuation from the Hinku Valley by helicopter costs $3,000–$5,000 and is not covered by the NMA permit. Appropriate travel insurance covering helicopter rescue from altitude (minimum $100,000 medical evacuation) is the only real protection available if you need to come off the mountain fast. Our team can advise on appropriate policies before departure.
